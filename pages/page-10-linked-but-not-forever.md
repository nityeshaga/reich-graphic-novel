## Goal of This Page

Continue the "inheritance gets complicated" thread from page 09. TWO concepts: (1) Sex-linked inheritance — genes on the X chromosome behave differently in males (XY) vs females (XX), explaining why color blindness is ~8% in males but <1% in females, and (2) Genotype vs phenotype — your DNA (genotype) and your visible traits (phenotype) aren't always the same.

This page follows naturally from codominance/incomplete dominance — we're still in the "Mendel was simple, reality is messier" arc.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "The X Factor"

Wide panel. A large X chromosome and a small Y chromosome side by side, drawn to relative scale. The X is BIG and covered in gene labels along its length. The Y is tiny with almost nothing on it.

Helix stands between them: "There's one more complication. The X chromosome is packed with genes. The Y chromosome is... not. If you're male (XY), you have only ONE X. Whatever's on it — that's what you get. No backup copy."

A simple example shown below: color blindness.
- Female (XX): needs TWO copies of the recessive allele to be color blind → rare (~0.5%)
- Male (XY): needs just ONE copy on his single X → common (~8%)

Handwritten: "This is why color blindness, hemophilia, and some muscular dystrophies are far more common in males."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — A Quick Visual

Two small figures:
- A woman labeled "Carrier (Xᶜ X)" — sees colors normally, but carries one copy of the color-blind allele
- Her son labeled "Color blind (Xᶜ Y)" — got the Xᶜ from mom, has no second X to compensate

Arrow from mother to son with label: "50% chance each son inherits the color-blind X"

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Reacts

Nityesh, intrigued.

Speech bubble: "So a woman can CARRY the allele without being affected, and pass it to her sons who DO get affected? That's why it runs in families on the mother's side?"

Helix: "Exactly. Now let me show you one more distinction — maybe the most subtle but most important one."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "Genotype vs Phenotype"

Two people standing side by side — both have brown eyes. They look similar. But above each person, a thought bubble reveals their genotype:

- Person 1: **BB** (two dominant alleles)
- Person 2: **Bb** (one dominant, one recessive — carrier)

They LOOK the same (same phenotype: brown eyes). But they CARRY different information (different genotypes).

Helix holds up two labels:
- "GENOTYPE = your actual DNA code (BB or Bb)"
- "PHENOTYPE = what shows up (brown eyes)"

Handwritten: "Same appearance. Different genetics. You can't always read the DNA from the outside."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh's Analogy

Nityesh, synthesizing.

Speech bubble: "So my genome is like a hand of cards I can't fully see. I know what I LOOK like — that's my phenotype. But I don't always know what I'm CARRYING — that's my genotype."

Helix: "Perfect analogy."

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — The Hook

Helix shifts to something bigger — transitioning from "complications of single genes" to the real game.

Speech bubble: "You now understand how single genes work — dominant, recessive, codominant, sex-linked. But here's the thing: most traits that matter aren't controlled by one gene. Height, skin color, disease risk — they're controlled by THOUSANDS."

Nityesh: "Thousands? How do you even study that?"

Helix: "You stop looking at individual genes. And you start looking at the whole orchestra."
