## Goal of This Page

Teach dominant vs recessive alleles and Mendel's First Law (segregation). TWO concepts: (1) some alleles mask others — dominant shows with one copy, recessive only shows with two copies, and (2) each parent randomly passes ONE of their two alleles to each offspring (50/50 chance). Punnett squares as comic panels.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Answer"

Wide panel. Three pea plants side by side, each with their allele combination shown as cards below them:

- **PP** (two purple cards) → Purple flower. Label: "Homozygous dominant"
- **Pp** (one purple, one white card) → Purple flower. Label: "Heterozygous"
- **pp** (two white cards) → White flower. Label: "Homozygous recessive"

Helix points at the middle plant: "See? One purple allele and one white allele... the flower is PURPLE. The purple allele is DOMINANT — it shows up even when there's only one copy. White only appears when BOTH copies are white."

Nityesh: "So the purple allele bosses the white one around?"

Helix: "Exactly. The dominant allele masks the recessive one."

### Slot 3-4 (top-right, merged 2 slots) — "The Punnett Square"

A Punnett square drawn as a 2×2 grid of small comic panels — each cell shows a tiny pea plant with its flower color:

Cross: **Pp × Pp** (both parents heterozygous)

|  | **P** | **p** |
|--|-------|-------|
| **P** | PP (purple) | Pp (purple) |
| **p** | Pp (purple) | pp (WHITE!) |

The pp cell is highlighted with a spotlight effect — the surprise white flower among three purple ones.

Helix circles the pp cell: "There she is. The hidden recessive, popping up in the third generation. Two purple-flowered parents... and one in four offspring is white."

Handwritten annotation: "Mendel's First Law (Segregation): Each parent randomly passes ONE of their two alleles. 50/50 chance."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "Skipping Generations"

A simple family tree panel — three generations of humans (not pea plants, making it personal):

- **Generation 1:** Brown-eyed grandmother (BB) × blue-eyed grandfather (bb)
- **Generation 2:** All brown-eyed children (Bb) — carriers of the blue allele
- **Generation 3:** Two Bb parents have children → one child has BLUE EYES (bb)

The blue-eyed grandchild and the blue-eyed grandfather are connected by a dotted line labeled "Same allele, two generations apart."

Helix: "Blue eyes skipped a generation. Both parents are brown-eyed. But they each secretly carry one blue allele. And by chance, their child got the blue from BOTH of them."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Connects

Nityesh, eyes lighting up with recognition.

Speech bubble: "So my blue-eyed cousin isn't a mystery? Both his parents just happened to carry a hidden 'b' allele?"

Helix: "Not a mystery. Math."

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — The Hook

Helix gestures at the pea plants behind them — all neatly purple or white, clean binary outcomes.

Speech bubble: "But Mendel only studied traits with two neat options — purple or white, tall or short. Real life isn't that clean, is it?"

Nityesh looks at his own skin tone, his hair texture — complex traits that clearly aren't binary.

Nityesh: "No... definitely not."
