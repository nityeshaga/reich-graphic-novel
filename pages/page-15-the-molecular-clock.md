## Goal of This Page

The molecular clock — the most important single tool in genetic history. TWO concepts: (1) Mutations accumulate at a roughly steady rate, so counting differences between two genomes estimates when they diverged, and (2) this is how scientists dated the human-Neanderthal split to ~500,000-750,000 years ago.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-3 (top row, merged 3 slots) — "The Clock"

Wide cinematic panel. The central visual: two timelines diverging from a common point, like a tuning fork. Both branches stretch to the right (toward the present). Along each branch, small star marks (mutations) appear at regular intervals — ticking like a clock.

Labels on the tuning fork:
- Common ancestor (at the fork point, far left)
- Branch 1: "Your lineage" — with mutation ticks accumulating
- Branch 2: "Another lineage" — with its own mutation ticks

At the tips (present day): count the total ticks between the two tips = time since divergence.

Large handwritten annotation: "More differences = longer separation. Fewer differences = recent split."

Helix stands at the fork point: "Mutations tick like a clock — roughly steady over long timescales. Count the differences between two genomes, and you can estimate when they last shared a common ancestor. No fossils needed."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — "The Numbers"

A comparison bar chart — visual, not just numbers:

- **Two random modern humans:** small bar (relatively few differences)
- **Modern human vs Neanderthal:** medium bar (~10× more differences)
- **Modern human vs chimpanzee:** large bar (~10× more again)

Handwritten: "The ratios tell the story. ~10× more different from Neanderthals than from each other. ~10× more from chimps than Neanderthals."
