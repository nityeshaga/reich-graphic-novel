## 4. TEACHING CLARITY

### Accurate and clear

- **Page 05 (DNA Replication):** Excellent coverage. Helicase, DNA polymerase, leading/lagging strand, Okazaki fragments, semi-conservative replication, proofreading — all accurate. The Meselson-Stahl sidebar is a nice touch for readers who want depth. The "3.8 million cell divisions per second" stat is correct and suitably mind-blowing.

- **Page 06 (Mitosis vs Meiosis):** The structural contrast is well-executed. The progression from "why meiosis must exist" (numbers game) to "how it works" (the shuffle) to "what it means" (8.4 million combinations) is logical. Crossing over is correctly placed in Meiosis I. The 2^23 calculation is correct.

- **Page 07 (Recombination as Clock):** The segment-length-as-clock concept is THE foundational technique for the rest of the book, and this page nails it. The Oase Cave Man example is well-chosen — "within 6 generations" makes the abstract concrete. The Queen Elizabeth II paradox (genealogy ≠ genetic inheritance) is a genuinely surprising fact that will stick with readers.

- **Page 08 (mtDNA and Y chromosome):** The three-timeline discordance setup is accurate and dramatically presented. Good.

### Science issues to address

**Page 05, Slot 4 — Leading/Lagging strand directionality:**
The spec says "DNA Polymerase can only build in one direction (5'→3')." This is correct. But the diagram description says the template strands run "3'→5' and 5'→3'" — this is the template orientation, and the new strand is built 5'→3' in both cases. The distinction between template direction and synthesis direction could confuse the image model or a reader. Recommend adding a small annotation: "Polymerase always READS 3'→5' on the template and BUILDS 5'→3' on the new strand" to disambiguate.

**Page 05, Slot 7 — Error rate claim:**
"Only ~1 error per replication (after proofreading and repair)" — this is too low. The actual error rate after proofreading by polymerase is about 1 per 10^7 bases, but AFTER mismatch repair it drops to about 1 per 10^9 to 10^10. For a 3.2 billion base genome, that's roughly 0.3-3 errors per replication — so "~1" is in the right ballpark. But the "1.2 million pages with one typo" line implies a lower rate than reality. The ~60-70 mutations per generation figure (used correctly on page 04) includes germline mutations accumulated over ~25-30 years of cell divisions. The per-replication and per-generation numbers serve different purposes. Recommend: keep "~1 error per replication" but add "(per cell division — germline cells divide many times before producing a sperm or egg, so ~60-70 mutations accumulate per generation)." This reconciles with page 04 and prevents a sharp reader from noticing the apparent contradiction.

**Page 07, Slot 4 — "~1-2 cuts per chromosome per generation":**
This is stated twice on the page (slots 1-3 and slot 4). The actual average crossover rate is ~1-2 per chromosome per meiosis, which is correct. However, the formula "Average segment length / expected cuts per generation = generations since mixture" is a simplification. Reich's actual method (ALDER, ROLLOFF, GLOBETROTTER) uses the DECAY of linkage disequilibrium, not a simple division. The simplification is fine for a graphic novel — but the annotation "This is how Reich dates EVERY admixture event in the book. It's not a metaphor — it's the actual method" oversells it. Recommend changing to: "This is the core PRINCIPLE behind Reich's dating methods. The actual math involves statistical decay patterns — but the intuition is exactly this: shorter segments = older mixture."

**Page 08, Slots 5-6 — Divergence dates:**
The spec gives:
- Nuclear DNA: 500,000-750,000 years (human-Neanderthal split)
- mtDNA: 300,000-400,000 years
- Y chromosome: 300,000-400,000 years

The nuclear DNA date is approximately correct (often cited as ~550,000-765,000 years). But the mtDNA divergence is usually cited as ~400,000-500,000 years (Posth et al. 2017), not 300,000-400,000. And the Y chromosome divergence, based on Petr et al. 2020, is cited at ~370,000 years — so 300,000-400,000 is reasonable for Y but the mtDNA figure should be revised upward to ~400,000-500,000 years to match the literature. The GAP between nuclear and uniparental dates is the real point — and it's still dramatic with corrected numbers.

**Page 08, Slots 5-6 — Helix's explanation:**
"The ONLY explanation: gene flow from modern humans INTO Neanderthals replaced their maternal and paternal lineages." This is presented as the ONLY explanation, but it's more accurately described as the LEADING hypothesis. The Posth et al. 2017 paper proposed this (replacement of early Neanderthal mtDNA by an African/early modern human lineage), but it's still debated. The "0.25% probability" figure seems to reference a specific statistical test — make sure this comes from a cited source. Recommend: "The most likely explanation: gene flow from an early modern human population INTO Neanderthals replaced their maternal and paternal lineages." Changing "ONLY" to "most likely" is more scientifically honest and, honestly, MORE intriguing — it leaves room for mystery.

### Potential confusion points

**Page 06, Slot 7 — "8.4 million combinations... before counting recombination":**
The spec correctly separates independent assortment (2^23) from recombination. But the phrasing "The true number of unique gametes is effectively infinite" is technically incorrect — it's astronomically large but finite. A better phrasing: "The true number of unique gametes is so large it's practically unlimited — every sperm and egg you'll ever produce will be different."

**Page 07, Slot 5 — Queen Elizabeth II ancestor count:**
"~16.7 million THEORETICAL ancestors at 24 generations... only ~1,751 contributed ANY DNA." The 16.7 million figure (2^24) is correct as the theoretical maximum. The ~1,751 figure appears to come from modeling work on expected genetic ancestors — this is a reasonable estimate but should be flagged as approximate. Recommend adding: "(estimated — the exact number depends on population structure and inbreeding in the royal lineage)."

Chapter 2 teaches the biology that makes those three histories possible (replication, meiosis, recombination, uniparental inheritance) and then DELIVERS the discordance in page 08.
