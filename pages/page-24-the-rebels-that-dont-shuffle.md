## Goal of This Page

The rebel lineages — mtDNA and Y chromosome. TWO concepts: (1) Mitochondrial DNA passes only through mothers, Y chromosome passes only through fathers — neither recombines, so they travel through time INTACT, changing only through mutations, and (2) The bombshell preview — because these two lineages DON'T get shuffled, they tell a COMPLETELY DIFFERENT story about human-Neanderthal divergence than the rest of the genome. This sets up the "three witnesses" framework for Chapter 5.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "Two Rebels"

Wide panel. Two parallel chains stretch backward in time (right to left), each made of linked figures:

**Top chain — MATERNAL LINE (warm coral/pink):**
A chain of female silhouettes linked together — daughter ← mother ← grandmother ← great-grandmother — stretching back into deep time. A small circular mitochondria icon travels with them, passing from each mother to the next. Label: "mtDNA — Mothers only. No father's DNA gets in."

**Bottom chain — PATERNAL LINE (deep blue):**
A chain of male silhouettes linked together — son ← father ← grandfather ← great-grandfather — stretching back in parallel. A Y-shaped icon travels with them. Label: "Y Chromosome — Fathers only. No mother's DNA gets in."

Between the two chains, a faint tangle of other chromosomes (the regular genome) being shuffled, mixed, recombined — chaotic and colorful. The two rebel chains pass through this chaos UNTOUCHED.

Helix: "Almost all your DNA gets shuffled every generation. But two pieces DON'T. Your mitochondrial DNA came from your mother, who got it from HER mother, who got it from HER mother — all the way back. And the Y chromosome passes father to son to son, equally unchanged."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "Only Mutations Change Them"

A single rebel chain (the mtDNA line) shown close up. It passes through many generations identically, but occasionally a small star appears — a mutation. The mutation persists in all descendants after it appears. Each mutation is a permanent marker on the chain.

Helix: "No recombination means no shuffling. The ONLY changes are mutations — rare, random typos that accumulate slowly over thousands of years. Each one is a permanent timestamp."

Handwritten: "mtDNA mutation rate: roughly one mutation every few thousand years. Y chromosome: ~1 every 150 years. Slow, steady, countable."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Processes

Nityesh, thinking it through carefully.

Speech bubble: "So these two lineages are like... unbroken recordings? The rest of the genome is a shuffled mosaic, but mtDNA and the Y chromosome are straight lines through time — you can trace them back like a thread?"

Helix: "Exactly. Two unbroken threads. Two independent witnesses to the past."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Bombshell"

Wide panel, dramatic. A visual of THREE witnesses on a witness stand — personified as characters:

1. **The Genome** (a large, complex mosaic figure — patchwork of many colors): representing the full nuclear genome, shuffled and recombined
2. **mtDNA** (a small, simple, single-color figure — coral/pink): representing the maternal line
3. **Y Chromosome** (a small, simple, single-color figure — blue with a Y shape): representing the paternal line

All three are pointing at a timeline on the wall behind them. But they're pointing at DIFFERENT DATES for the same event ("When did humans and Neanderthals diverge?").

The Genome points to one date. mtDNA points to a date MUCH FURTHER BACK. The Y chromosome points to yet another date.

Helix (serious, dramatic): "Here's the bombshell. When scientists asked 'When did humans and Neanderthals split apart?', these three witnesses gave DIFFERENT ANSWERS. The full genome says one thing. The maternal line says another. And the paternal line says something else entirely."

Nityesh, leaning forward, gripped.

Speech bubble: "Different answers? How is that possible? They're all part of the same person's DNA — they should tell the same story."

Helix: "You'd think so. But they don't. And the reason WHY they disagree is one of the most important insights in David Reich's entire career."
