## Goal of This Page

The second half of the mutations intro, split from the original page 13. TWO concepts: (1) Frameshifts — deletions/insertions that aren't multiples of 3 scramble everything downstream, and (2) Mutation rate — about 60-70 new mutations per generation, most in non-coding regions with no effect. This page bridges to the molecular clock on the next page.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Frameshift"

Wide panel. The most dramatic mutation type, shown with the sentence analogy:

**Original:** "THE BIG RED FOX RAN FAR"
**One letter deleted:** "THE BI GRE DFO XRA NFA R"

Every word after the deletion is garbled. The typography visually disintegrates — each word more chaotic than the last, letters at wrong angles, fragments falling apart.

Helix: "Delete ONE letter — not three, just one — and every word downstream is scrambled. A FRAMESHIFT. Almost always devastating."

Handwritten: "Insertions or deletions that aren't in multiples of 3 shift the reading frame. Every codon after the change is wrong."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "Bigger Rearrangements"

Quick visual showing larger structural mutations — simple, clean diagrams:
- A section of chromosome being DUPLICATED (copy-paste)
- A section being INVERTED (flipped)
- A section jumping to another chromosome (TRANSLOCATION)

Helix: "Beyond single-letter typos, whole sections can be duplicated, flipped, or relocated. Rarer, but when they happen, they can reshuffle large parts of the genome."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Absorbs

Nityesh, slightly awed by the range of things that can go wrong.

Speech bubble: "So a typo can be invisible, or it can wreck an entire protein, or it can rearrange a whole chromosome. That's a huge range."

Helix: "And here's the crucial number..."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Rate"

A visual: a long ribbon of DNA. Across the entire ribbon, ~60-70 tiny stars appear — scattered, most in the gray (non-coding) regions, a few in the colored (gene) regions.

Helix: "About 60-70 new mutations per generation. That's roughly 1-2 per year. MOST land in non-coding regions and have zero effect. The cell's proofreading catches the vast majority of errors — only the ones that slip through become permanent."

Handwritten: "Of ~60 mutations per generation, only a handful affect genes. And of those, most are neutral."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Makes the Connection

The key moment — Nityesh independently deducing the molecular clock, connecting what he learned here to the bigger picture.

Speech bubble: "So mutations create new alleles. The first blue-eye allele was a mutation in one person, thousands of years ago, that happened to change eye color. And if these mutations tick along at a steady rate..."

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — The Hook

Nityesh's eyes widen — he's getting there on his own.

Nityesh: "...you could count the mutations between two genomes and figure out how long ago they diverged. Like a clock."

Helix: (beaming with pride) "You just invented the molecular clock. Let me show you how powerful it is."
