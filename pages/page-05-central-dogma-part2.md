## Goal of This Page

Teach TRANSLATION — the second half of the Central Dogma. Two concepts: (1) the ribosome reads mRNA three letters at a time (codons), each coding for one amino acid, and (2) the genetic code is nearly universal across ALL life — evidence of common ancestry. The reader should understand how a string of letters becomes a physical protein.

This page continues the factory metaphor from the previous page. The mRNA has arrived at the factory floor. Now we watch the assembly line in action.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-3 (top row, merged 3 slots) — "The Assembly Line"

A wide, detailed factory floor scene — the visual centerpiece of this page. The RIBOSOME is drawn as a large, impressive assembly machine sitting on the factory floor (cytoplasm). The mRNA feeds through it like a conveyor belt tape.

The process, shown step by step in the panel from left to right:

1. **mRNA enters the ribosome** — the tape feeds in from the left, with bases visible (e.g., AUG, UUU, GCA, UCC, AGU...)

2. **Reading three at a time** — a magnifying lens built into the ribosome highlights THREE bases at a time. The current window shows "AUG" with a label: "Each group of 3 = one CODON"

3. **tRNA delivery trucks arrive** — small truck-shaped characters (transfer RNA) roll up, each carrying a colored ball (amino acid) on their flatbed. Each truck has an "anti-codon" license plate that matches the codon being read. The truck for AUG carries a green ball labeled "Met" (methionine — the start amino acid).

4. **The growing chain** — on the right side of the ribosome, a chain of colored balls (amino acids) extends outward, getting longer. Each ball is a different color. The chain begins to curl and fold. Label: "GROWING POLYPEPTIDE CHAIN"

5. **The finished product** — at the far right, the chain has folded into a compact 3D shape. Label: "FINISHED PROTEIN (folds into a specific 3D shape)"

Helix stands on top of the ribosome like a foreman overseeing the line, clipboard in hand: "Three letters at a time. Each triplet codes for one amino acid building block. Chain them together, fold them up — that's a protein."
