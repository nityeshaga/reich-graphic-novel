## Goal of This Page

Show that real inheritance is more complex than Mendel's clean dominant/recessive — and that the COMPLEX cases are what matter for understanding human populations. Three ideas: codominance (both alleles expressed), polygenic traits (hundreds of genes per trait), and sex-linked inheritance. Each one should be introduced through a concrete, relatable example the reader already has intuitions about.

This page bridges from "genetics as a monk's pea garden" to "genetics as a tool for reading human history."

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "When Both Alleles Speak"

A wide panel introducing codominance through blood types — a system everyone has encountered.

**The Blood Type Story:**
A visual showing four blood types with simple icons (red blood cells with different surface markers):

- **Type A:** Cell with A markers. Genotype: I^A I^A or I^A i
- **Type B:** Cell with B markers. Genotype: I^B I^B or I^B i
- **Type AB:** Cell with BOTH A and B markers. Genotype: I^A I^B
- **Type O:** Cell with NO markers. Genotype: ii

The key visual: Type AB blood cell shown with BOTH A and B markers clearly visible on its surface. Neither masks the other.

Helix: "Mendel's peas were either purple OR white. But blood type A and B are CODOMINANT — if you have one of each, you get BOTH. Neither one wins."

Below: "Your blood type is one of the first things genetic ancestry tests can tell you. Different populations have very different blood type frequencies — a clue to migration history."

Nityesh: "So that's why I'm AB — one allele from each parent, and both show up."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "The Blend"

Incomplete dominance — a quick, clean concept:

Red snapdragon flower × White snapdragon flower → PINK flower

"Some alleles don't fully dominate OR share equally. Instead you get a blend — the heterozygote looks like a middle ground."

Visual: three flowers in a row — red, pink, white. The pink one has a speech bubble: "I'm the compromise."

Annotation: "Mendel's peas didn't show this because the alleles he studied happened to be fully dominant. He was lucky — or very careful in choosing his traits."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — "Mendel's Two Laws (Quick Reference)"

A clean reference panel — the two laws stated simply with small visuals:

**Law 1 — Segregation:**
"Each parent randomly passes ONE of their two alleles to each child."
Visual: a coin flip — heads = allele A, tails = allele a. Pure 50/50 chance.

**Law 2 — Independent Assortment:**
"Genes on DIFFERENT chromosomes are inherited independently. Your eye color gene and your earlobe gene shuffle separately."
Visual: two slot machine reels spinning independently — each reel is a different chromosome.

Small note: "Exception: genes on the SAME chromosome can be 'linked' and travel together. This matters later."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Big One: Polygenic Traits"

The most important concept on this page — and the bridge to everything David Reich does.

**The Problem With Simple Genetics:**
"Mendel's traits had one gene with two alleles. Eye color? Sort of. But HEIGHT? SKIN COLOR? DISEASE RISK? These are influenced by HUNDREDS or even THOUSANDS of genes, each contributing a tiny effect."

**The Bell Curve Visual:**
A large bell curve showing height distribution in a population. Along the bottom, hundreds of tiny gene markers — each one nudging the person slightly taller or shorter. The SUM of all these tiny nudges creates the familiar bell-shaped distribution.

"This is why siblings can be very different heights even with the same parents. Each got a different random draw of hundreds of height-related alleles."

**The Bridge to Reich:**
A crucial callout box: "David Reich doesn't study single genes. He studies ALLELE FREQUENCIES — how common each allele version is across entire populations. When populations mix, their allele frequencies blend. When they're isolated, allele frequencies drift apart. THAT'S how you read history from DNA."

Helix: "Mendel counted peas. Reich counts allele frequencies across millions of people. Same principle, vastly larger scale."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — "The X Factor"

Sex-linked inheritance — why the X chromosome is special:

A simple diagram:
- Females: XX (two X chromosomes — a backup copy)
- Males: XY (one X, one Y — no backup)

"Color blindness is on the X chromosome. If a woman has one color-blind allele, her other X compensates. If a man has it — no backup. The trait shows."

Stats: "Color blindness: ~8% of men, <1% of women. Same gene, different odds."

Annotation: "The X chromosome spends 2/3 of its time in female bodies — because females have two X chromosomes and males have one, any given X is twice as likely to be in a female body. This means it experiences different evolutionary pressures — and Reich uses this to detect whether ancient migrations were male-biased or female-biased."

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — Hook Panel

Nityesh, looking satisfied — the pieces clicking into place:

Nityesh, looking at his hands: "If every population carries different allele frequencies... and genes change over time... then why are some populations SO different from each other? What's actually driving that?"

Helix, with an excited expression, gesturing dramatically:

"Three forces: mutations CREATE new variants. Natural selection FILTERS them. And random drift SHUFFLES them. Those three forces write the story of human history — and everything David Reich studies starts on the next page."

Bold text at bottom: "Next: When the Code Changes"

## Visual Design Notes

- WARM CREAM/CHALKBOARD BACKGROUND — Helix teaching page
- The blood type panel in slots 1-2 should use clear, distinct colors for each type. The AB cell with both markers is the key visual — make both markers clearly visible on the same cell.
- The bell curve in slots 5-6 is the CONCEPTUAL PEAK of the page — it bridges from "genetics is about peas" to "genetics is about populations." Make it beautiful, not clinical. The hundreds of tiny gene markers along the bottom should feel overwhelming in a good way — conveying the sheer scale of polygenic traits.
- The X-linked panel should clearly show WHY males are more affected — the single X chromosome with no backup copy.
- The hook at the end should feel like a natural escalation — from "how genes are inherited" to "how genes CHANGE" — setting up the mutations/selection/drift page.
- Keep the pacing relaxed. This page has more concepts than page 3, but each one gets a full panel. No concept should feel rushed.
