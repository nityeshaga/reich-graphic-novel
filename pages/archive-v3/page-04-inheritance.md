## Goal of This Page

Teach JUST the core of Mendelian genetics: alleles, dominant vs recessive, and Punnett squares. The reader should walk away understanding one key idea — you carry TWO copies of each gene, one from each parent, and some versions mask others. That's it. This is the "why do I look like my parents (but not exactly)" page.

Give each concept ROOM to breathe. The previous page taught the central dogma — the reader is still processing. Go slow. Use Mendel's story as an emotional anchor.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-3 (top row, merged 3 slots) — "The Monk and the Peas"

A WIDE, warm panoramic illustration. Gregor Mendel in his sun-dappled monastery garden in Brno, kneeling among tall pea plants. Purple and white flowers surround him. He holds a small notebook with tally marks. The setting should feel peaceful, curious, unhurried — a man patiently counting peas for eight years.

Caption: "1866. Gregor Mendel. An Augustinian friar who spent eight years crossing pea plants and counting the results. He discovered the laws of inheritance — 90 years before anyone knew DNA existed."

Helix sits on a pea pod nearby, watching Mendel work.

Below the illustration, the SINGLE KEY CONCEPT of this section:

**"You carry TWO copies of every gene — one from mom, one from dad."**

A simple visual: a pair of chromosomes shown side by side — one labeled "from Mom" and one labeled "from Dad." On each chromosome, a small colored segment (the gene) is highlighted. Mom's copy is purple (P). Dad's copy is white (p).

"These different versions are called ALLELES. Think of them as two competing voices — each parent contributes one, and the gene has to decide which one to listen to."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — "The Vocabulary"

A clean, simple reference panel — the ONLY jargon on this page, clearly defined:

**Allele** = a version of a gene. Like "purple flower" vs "white flower" — same gene, different versions.

**Homozygous** = both copies the same
- PP (two purple) or pp (two white)
- Illustrated with two matching colored blocks

**Heterozygous** = two different copies
- Pp (one purple, one white)
- Illustrated with two different colored blocks

Helix: "These words will come back over and over. If you remember nothing else: alleles are versions, and you always have two."

### Slots 5-7 (bottom-left, merged 3 slots) — "Why Traits Skip Generations"

The emotional and conceptual heart of the page. A THREE-GENERATION family story told through a Punnett square:

**STEP 1 — The Parents:**
Two brown-eyed parents. Both heterozygous (Bb). Each carries one brown allele (B, dominant) and one blue allele (b, recessive). Their eyes are brown because B masks b.

Visual: two people with brown eyes. Inside each, a B and a b allele shown as colored cards — the B card is bigger/brighter, overshadowing the b.

Nityesh: "Wait — they BOTH have a hidden blue-eye allele?"

**STEP 2 — The Punnett Square:**
A large, colorful, CLEAR Punnett square:

```
       B      b
  B   BB     Bb
  b   Bb     bb
```

Each box shows the result with a small eye illustration:
- BB = brown eyes
- Bb = brown eyes (B masks b)
- Bb = brown eyes
- bb = BLUE EYES — the hidden allele from BOTH parents combined

Bold: "1 in 4 chance. The trait SKIPPED a generation. The blue-eye allele was always there — just hidden behind brown."

Small asterisk note: "(Simplified — eye color actually involves multiple genes, as we'll see on the next page. But the principle is exactly right.)"

**STEP 3 — The Reveal:**
A three-generation family tree:
- Grandparents: one with blue eyes (bb)
- Parents: both brown-eyed (Bb) — carrying the hidden allele
- Child: blue eyes (bb) — "Where did THAT come from?!"

Annotation: "This is dominant (B = brown) vs recessive (b = blue). The dominant allele's trait shows even with just one copy. The recessive only shows when BOTH copies are recessive."

Helix: "Mendel figured this out from PEAS. Purple vs white flowers. Tall vs short plants. Round vs wrinkled seeds. The same pattern, every time — 3:1 ratio."

Helix grins: "Great question. Mendel's peas were the simple case. Reality is messier — and the messy parts are exactly what matters for understanding human populations."

## Visual Design Notes

- WARM CREAM/CHALKBOARD BACKGROUND throughout — Helix teaching page
- Mendel's garden in slots 1-3 should be the WARMEST, most inviting illustration in the chapter so far. This is a human story — a patient monk counting peas — before it's a science lesson. Let the setting do emotional work.
- The Punnett square in slots 5-7 should be BIG and CLEAR — not a tiny diagram. Each box should be large enough to include the eye illustration. The 3:1 pattern should be visually obvious.
- The three-generation family tree should feel personal and relatable — real people with real eye colors, not stick figures or abstract diagrams.
- This page deliberately has FEWER concepts than the storyboard originally planned. The goal is depth over breadth — one idea (dominant/recessive inheritance) understood completely rather than six ideas understood superficially.
- Helix should be patient and encouraging throughout — the pace should feel like a good tutor, not a textbook
