## Goal of This Page

How recombination creates a readable timestamp in the genome. TWO concepts: (1) Your genome is a mosaic — segments from different ancestors get SHORTER with each generation of recombination, so long foreign segments mean recent mixture and short fragments mean ancient mixture, and (2) The Oase Cave man example — a 40,000-year-old human skeleton with Neanderthal DNA spanning nearly 1/3 of one chromosome, proving he had a Neanderthal ancestor within just 4-6 generations. This is how Reich dates admixture events.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Chopping Effect"

Wide panel. A visual sequence showing ONE foreign segment of DNA (colored bright red against a blue background genome) getting progressively chopped across generations:

- **Generation 1:** One long red segment — takes up nearly half the chromosome. Label: "Your parent was the source"
- **Generation 3:** Two medium red segments on different chromosomes. Label: "Grandchild"
- **Generation 6:** Several small red fragments scattered across multiple chromosomes. Label: "6 generations later"
- **Generation 20:** Tiny red specks, barely visible, scattered widely. Label: "20 generations"

An arrow runs left to right with the label: "TIME →"

Helix: "Every generation, recombination CHOPS foreign segments shorter. The math is predictable — the average segment length halves roughly every generation. So if you find a long foreign segment in someone's genome..."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "Reading the Clock"

A visual "ruler" showing the relationship between segment length and time:

- LONG segment → RECENT mixture (few generations ago)
- MEDIUM segment → MODERATE mixture (~10-20 generations)
- SHORT fragments → ANCIENT mixture (hundreds of generations)

Helix: "...you can CALCULATE when the mixture happened. Long segments = recent. Short fragments = ancient. Recombination is a clock."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Connects

Nityesh, eyes lighting up with understanding.

Speech bubble: "So when Reich analyzes someone's genome and finds segments of, say, Neanderthal DNA — the LENGTH of those segments tells him how many generations ago the interbreeding happened?"

Helix: "Exactly. And he found the most extraordinary example in a cave in Romania."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Oase Cave Man"

Wide, dramatic panel. A cave setting — dim, atmospheric. A human jawbone rests on sediment (this is the real Oase 1 fossil, ~40,000 years old). Above the jawbone, a visualization of the individual's genome — a long chromosome ribbon.

The striking visual: nearly ONE-THIRD of one chromosome is colored in a distinct Neanderthal color (deep green) — an enormous, unbroken block. The rest of the genome has much smaller, scattered Neanderthal fragments.

Helix (with reverence): "The Oase Cave man. Romania. 40,000 years old. When they sequenced his genome, they found a Neanderthal segment spanning NEARLY A THIRD of one chromosome. A block that long could only mean one thing..."

Handwritten (large): "A Neanderthal ancestor within 4-6 generations. His great-great-great-grandparent was a Neanderthal."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh's Reaction

Nityesh, stunned, almost breathless.

Speech bubble: "His GREAT-GREAT-GREAT-GRANDPARENT. Not some abstract ancient event thousands of years ago — a real, traceable family connection. He could have KNOWN the Neanderthal ancestor's grandchildren."

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — The Hook

Nityesh, still processing the Oase revelation, then a thought hits him.

Speech bubble: "Wait. All of this — the shuffling, the chopping, the timestamps — it only works because recombination mixes everything up each generation. But what if some DNA DIDN'T get shuffled? Would it just... stay intact forever?"

Helix (with a knowing look): "Now you're asking the right question."
