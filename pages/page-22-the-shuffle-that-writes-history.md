## Goal of This Page

Crossing over (recombination) during meiosis. TWO concepts: (1) Homologous chromosomes physically swap segments during meiosis I — this is crossing over, happening ~71 times per generation across the genome, and (2) The result — each gamete is a UNIQUE patchwork quilt of grandparental DNA, which is why siblings (who share the same parents) are genetically different from each other.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Crossover"

Wide, dramatic panel — the visual centerpiece of this page. Two chromosomes side by side — one colored BLUE (from dad's mom, i.e., paternal grandmother) and one colored ORANGE (from dad's dad, i.e., paternal grandfather). They are pressed close together, aligned.

At two points along their length, the chromosomes have CROSSED — the blue and orange strands intertwine, forming an X shape at each crossing point. The visual is dynamic, almost electric — little spark effects at the crossover points.

After the crossover: the chromosomes separate, but now each one is a PATCHWORK — the blue chromosome has an orange segment spliced in, and the orange chromosome has a blue segment spliced in.

Helix: "Crossing over. During meiosis, the chromosome you got from your grandmother and the one from your grandfather physically TOUCH — and swap segments. They trade pieces of themselves."

Handwritten: "~71 crossover events per generation across all chromosomes. Every gamete is a remix."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "The Patchwork Result"

A single chromosome shown after multiple crossover events — it's no longer one solid color. It's a patchwork: blue-orange-blue-orange, with segments of varying lengths. Each segment traces back to a different grandparent.

Helix: "The result? Each chromosome in the gamete is no longer 'grandma's' or 'grandpa's.' It's a BRAND NEW combination — a patchwork that never existed before."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Absorbs

Nityesh, staring at the patchwork chromosome, connecting dots.

Speech bubble: "So by the time my dad makes a sperm cell, his chromosomes have been cut and re-stitched from HIS parents' DNA. And my mom's egg is the same — remixed from HER parents. And then THOSE combine to make me..."

Helix: "You are a remix of a remix."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "Why Siblings Are Different"

Wide panel. A family diagram: two parents at the top. Below them, four children — each represented by a horizontal genome ribbon. Each child's ribbon is a DIFFERENT patchwork pattern of the same four grandparental colors (blue, orange, green, purple — one per grandparent).

The patchwork patterns are visibly different across siblings — different segments, different lengths, different arrangements. But all four patterns use the same four source colors.

Helix: "Same parents. Same four grandparents. But because crossing over shuffles differently EVERY time, each child gets a unique patchwork. That's why you and your siblings share about 50% of your DNA — but never the SAME 50%."

Handwritten: "Full siblings: ~50% shared DNA. But WHICH 50% is different every time."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh's Question

Speech bubble: "Okay, so every generation, the DNA gets chopped up and reassembled. That's why siblings look different. But you said this is what David Reich STUDIES. How does a shuffle help him read ANCESTRY?"

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — The Hook

Helix, one arm raised, about to deliver the key insight.

Speech bubble: "Because the shuffle doesn't just make siblings different. It writes a TIMESTAMP into your genome. The longer ago something happened, the more the shuffle has had time to chop it up. And Reich learned to read those timestamps."
