## Goal of This Page

Teach DNA sequencing (reading fragments), alignment (mapping to a reference), coverage (how many reads), genome comparison (counting differences = measuring time), and the Four Population Test (the workhorse statistical method). Then introduce the industrialization of ancient DNA — Reich's "genomics factory." The reader should understand how raw DNA fragments become publishable genetic history.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "Reading the Fragments"

Wide panel. A visual showing the sequencing process as a JIGSAW PUZZLE:

**LEFT — The Fragments:** Millions of tiny DNA fragments (30-80 base pairs each) scattered randomly — like puzzle pieces dumped from a box. Each fragment is color-coded by its bases.

**CENTER — The Machine:** A next-generation sequencing chip — shown as a glowing grid with millions of tiny wells, each containing one fragment being read simultaneously. "Millions of fragments read AT THE SAME TIME on a single chip."

**RIGHT — The Alignment:** Each tiny fragment is mapped onto a LONG reference genome (the human reference, ~3.2 billion bases) — like fitting puzzle pieces into the correct position on the board. Some areas have many overlapping fragments (good coverage); some have gaps.

Annotation: "Each fragment is read multiple times for accuracy, then aligned to the reference genome to find where it belongs."

Helix: "Imagine shredding a million-page book, then reassembling it by matching fragments to a complete copy. That's sequencing."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "Coverage"

A simple diagram showing what "coverage" means:

A section of the reference genome shown as a horizontal bar. Above it, overlapping fragments stacked at different depths:

**1x coverage:** Each position read ONCE. Sparse, unreliable. Gaps visible. Label: "Most ancient genomes: 0.5-5x. We work with what we get."

**30x coverage:** Dense stacking, every position covered many times. Label: "Modern clinical standard. Very reliable."

"The more times each position is read, the more confident the result. Ancient DNA usually gives low coverage — so every fragment matters."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — "Counting Differences"

The molecular clock in ACTION — connecting back to page 04:

Two genome bars side by side, with small colored dots marking positions where they differ:

**Two random modern humans:** ~3 million differences (small number of dots)
**Modern human vs Neanderthal:** Significantly more differences — roughly 2-3x the within-human rate per base pair (many more dots)

"More differences = longer time since common ancestor."

The molecular clock equation (callback to page 04): "Differences ÷ Mutation rate = Time"

Helix: "The same molecular clock from Chapter 1 — but now applied to REAL ancient genomes."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Four Population Test"

THE WORKHORSE METHOD — the test that proved Neanderthal interbreeding.

A clear, visual explanation:

Four population icons arranged in a diamond:
- Top: **Chimpanzee** (outgroup — the reference point)
- Left: **African** (no Neanderthal ancestry expected)
- Right: **European** (potential Neanderthal ancestry)
- Bottom: **Neanderthal** (the ancient sample)

Arrows showing mutation-matching rates. The KEY comparison:

"If Europeans share MORE mutations with Neanderthals than Africans do → interbreeding happened."

"When this test was applied to Neanderthal and modern human genomes, the result was unambiguous. But that story — and the drama of scientists who couldn't believe their own data — comes in the next chapter."

Annotation: "This test doesn't require a complete genome. It works with fragments. That's why it was the first method capable of answering the Neanderthal interbreeding question — it could handle messy, incomplete ancient data."

## Visual Design Notes

- CLEAN WHITE/COOL BACKGROUND — interview page style
- The jigsaw puzzle metaphor in slots 1-2 should make sequencing INTUITIVE — fragments as puzzle pieces, the reference genome as the puzzle board
- Coverage (slot 3) is a dry concept — keep the visual simple and scannable. The key insight is: low coverage = less certain, and ancient DNA usually has low coverage.
- The Four Population Test in slots 5-6 is one of the most important concepts in the book — it needs to be crystal clear. The diamond arrangement of four populations should be immediately readable. The "one in a quadrillion" stat should feel DRAMATIC.
- The factory in slot 7 should feel like a deliberate contrast to the clean room of page 13 — the same reverence for the material, but at industrial scale.
- The hook into page 15 (the Denisovan finger bone) should feel like a thriller cliffhanger — "something nobody expected" + "unlike anything ever seen."
