## Characters

**Nityesh:** A pixel-art style young Indian man with distinctive big curly black hair, rectangular glasses, light stubble, white t-shirt, and beaded necklace. Expressive cartoon eyes. The curly hair is his most defining visual feature — big, voluminous, immediately recognizable. Pixel-art aesthetic — visible chunky pixels, retro game feel, but clearly this specific person.

**Helix:** A small, friendly, anthropomorphic double-helix DNA strand. Coral/salmon pink strands with teal/cyan rungs (base pairs) connecting them. Expressive cartoon eyes near the top, small stubby arms and legs. Approachable, curious, slightly nerdy. Standard comic style (NOT pixel-art). About 1/4 of Nityesh's height — a small companion character.

## Goal of This Page

DNA replication — the copy machine that makes cell division possible. TWO concepts: (1) Why replication matters — every time a cell divides, it must copy all 3.2 billion base pairs with astonishing accuracy (~1 error per billion bases), and (2) How the machinery works — helicase unzips, DNA polymerase reads and builds the complementary strand, proofreading enzymes fix mistakes, and each new double helix keeps one original strand (semi-conservative replication). This opens Chapter 4 and sets up the question: what are cells dividing FOR?

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Scale of the Job"

Wide establishing panel. Helix stands in front of an enormous scroll that stretches off both edges of the panel — representing the full 3.2 billion base pairs unrolled. Tiny hash marks along the scroll suggest the immense length. A magnifying glass hovers over one small section, showing individual A-T and C-G pairs.

Helix (arms spread wide): "Before ANY cell can divide, it has to copy ALL of this. 3.2 billion base pairs. Every. Single. One."

Handwritten annotation near the scroll: "If you printed your DNA as text, it would fill 262,000 pages. And the copy machine reproduces it in about 8 hours."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "The Error Rate"

A visual comparison. On the left, a stack of 1 billion letters (represented as a towering column of text). One letter near the top is circled in red — the single error.

Helix: "The error rate? About one mistake per BILLION bases copied. That's like typing out the entire Encyclopedia Britannica 300 times and making ONE typo."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Reacts

Nityesh, genuinely impressed, eyebrows raised.

Speech bubble: "One in a billion? How is ANYTHING that accurate?"

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Machinery"

Wide diagram panel showing the replication fork in action — dynamic, mechanical, like a factory floor. The double helix is being pulled apart at a Y-shaped fork:

- **Helicase** (drawn as a small wedge-shaped worker character): prying the two strands apart at the fork, breaking hydrogen bonds between base pairs. Label: "Helicase — the unzipper"
- **DNA Polymerase** (drawn as a larger worker character riding along each strand): reading the template strand and placing complementary bases one by one. Label: "DNA Polymerase — the builder"
- **Proofreader** (a small character with a magnifying glass following behind polymerase): checking each newly placed base, occasionally removing and replacing one. Label: "Proofreading — the spell-checker"

The two new double helices emerging from the fork each glow slightly — one strand old (darker), one strand new (brighter) in each.

Helix: "Helicase unzips. Polymerase reads each base and adds its complement — A pairs with T, C pairs with G. And a built-in proofreader follows behind, catching mistakes."

Handwritten: "Semi-conservative: each new double helix keeps one ORIGINAL strand. Half old, half new."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — "Semi-Conservative"

A clean, simple diagram. One parent double helix (both strands in dark blue) at the top. An arrow points down to two daughter double helices — each has one dark blue strand (original) and one light blue strand (new). The visual makes "semi-conservative" instantly intuitive.

Helix: "Each copy keeps one original strand as a template. That's why it's called semi-conservative — half is conserved, half is new."

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — The Hook

Nityesh, processing, then a new question forms.

Speech bubble: "Okay, so the copy machine is incredible. But WHY does a cell need to copy itself? When does this actually happen?"

Helix (grinning): "Two reasons. One is boring but essential. The other is the most important thing David Reich studies."
