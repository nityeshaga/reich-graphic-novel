Wide cinematic panel. A visual recap — a TIMELINE flowing left to right across the panel, showing the progression of ideas through Chapter 2:

And that comparing allele frequencies between populations is the fundamental tool of genetic history."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Reflects

Nityesh, sitting casually, relaxed — the posture of someone who's absorbed something and is now thinking about it rather than learning it.

Speech bubble: "So inheritance isn't simple — it's layers of complexity. But it all still comes back to alleles. Different versions of genes."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Question"

Nityesh leans forward, genuinely curious. A thought forms.

Speech bubble: "But WHERE do different alleles come from? Someone had to have the FIRST blue-eye allele, right? At some point, every human had brown eyes, and then... something changed?"

Helix: "Exactly right. Something changed in ONE person's DNA. A mistake during copying. And that mistake — that MUTATION — created a new allele that didn't exist before."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — The Connection

Helix, connecting it forward with purpose.

Speech bubble: "And here's why this matters for the podcast: David Reich uses the RATE at which these mistakes happen as a CLOCK. Count the mutations between two genomes, and you can measure the time since they diverged."

Nityesh: "Like counting tree rings?"

Helix: "Better. Tree rings only tell you the age of one tree. The molecular clock tells you when ANY two lineages last shared an ancestor."

Nityesh stands up from where he's sitting, energized, ready for the next section.

Helix hops forward, leading the way out of the monastery garden — the warm garden tones beginning to shift, the setting subtly changing to something more dynamic.

Helix: "Follow me. This is where biology becomes history."
