## Goal of This Page

Teach ONE core concept with dedicated breathing room: allele frequency — the proportion of a population carrying a particular allele. This is THE concept that unlocks the rest of the book. David Reich's entire body of work is built on comparing allele frequencies between populations. The page should make the reader understand: (1) what allele frequency means, (2) how to read it visually, and (3) why it's the tool geneticists actually use instead of looking at individual genes.

This page should feel like the climax of Chapter 2 — the moment where all the genetics basics snap into focus and point toward what comes next.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "Forget the Individual"

Wide panel. Setup: a single person standing alone, with a thought bubble showing their genotype for a particular gene — two colored dots representing their two alleles: one blue, one red.

Helix stands next to the person: "You have two copies of every gene. But knowing YOUR genotype doesn't tell a scientist much. What matters is the PATTERN across an entire population."

The single person is drawn small. Behind them, a crowd of silhouettes begins to appear — dozens, then hundreds.

Handwritten annotation: "Individual genotype = interesting to YOU. Population-wide pattern = interesting to SCIENCE."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "Counting Alleles"

A simple, clear diagram. A small group of 10 people arranged in a circle. Each person has a pair of colored dots above their head — their two alleles for one gene. Blue dot = allele A, red dot = allele B.

Count visible in the diagram:
- 14 blue dots (allele A) out of 20 total = 70%
- 6 red dots (allele B) out of 20 total = 30%

Helix points at the count: "20 alleles in total (10 people × 2 copies each). 14 are blue, 6 are red. The ALLELE FREQUENCY of blue is 70%."

Handwritten: "Allele frequency = how common a particular version of a gene is in a group. Just count and divide."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — Nityesh Checks His Understanding

Nityesh, working it out with his fingers, counting.

Speech bubble: "So allele frequency is literally just... what fraction of the group has a particular version? That's it?"

Helix: "That's it. Simple to define. Incredibly powerful to use."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "Two Populations, Two Fingerprints"

Wide panel. The key visual. Two large circles side by side, labeled "Population A" and "Population B." Each circle is filled with colored dots in different proportions:

- **Population A:** ~70% blue dots, ~30% red dots
- **Population B:** ~40% blue dots, ~60% red dots

Same gene. Same two alleles. But different frequencies.

Helix stands between the two circles, arms outstretched: "Same alleles exist in both groups. But the PROPORTIONS are different. Population A has mostly blue. Population B has mostly red. This difference in proportions — this is what David Reich reads."

Below the circles, a small additional visual: a row of 5 genes, each showing different frequency bars for Population A vs Population B. Like a barcode — each gene has a slightly different ratio.

Handwritten: "Now imagine doing this for THOUSANDS of genes. The pattern of frequencies across all those genes becomes a fingerprint for the population. That's what Reich compares."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — "Why Frequencies Change"

Helix, adding one more layer — the dynamic nature of allele frequencies.

Speech bubble: "And here's the thing — allele frequencies DON'T stay the same. They shift over time. A version that was rare can become common. A version that was common can disappear. When allele frequencies change across generations, that IS evolution."

Handwritten: "Evolution = changes in allele frequency over time. That's the technical definition."

Nityesh leans back, slightly overwhelmed but in a good way. He looks at Helix with an expression that's half awe, half "wait, how did we get here?"

Speech bubble: "Hold on. We started this chapter with a monk counting peas. Now we're talking about population fingerprints and evolution. How did we get from THERE to HERE?"
