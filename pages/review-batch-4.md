### Weak panels — flagged with alternatives

**Page 13, Slots 5-6 ("From Bone to DNA"):**
Issue: A four-step production line flowing left to right is functional but visually generic — it reads like a textbook infographic. For a merged two-slot panel (the largest format available), this is underusing the space. Each step is described with nearly identical visual weight, creating flatness.
Alternative: Frame the four steps as a ZOOM-IN sequence instead of a left-to-right flow. Start with the visible bone (macro), zoom into the powder (micro), zoom into the molecular level showing silica beads grabbing DNA strands (nano), and end with the glowing vial (back to macro). This "powers of ten" approach adds visual drama — the reader physically travels from the visible world into the molecular world and back. Each step has a different SCALE, not just a different action. The output vial should feel precious — maybe Helix holding it reverently.

**Page 13, Slot 7 ("The Contamination War"):**

This is a lot of information for one panel — the "wristband at a concert" metaphor for barcoding is clever but it's buried alongside the C-to-T damage pattern explanation.
Alternative: Pick ONE solution as the visual focus and relegate the other to an annotation. The C-to-T damage pattern is more visually interesting and more important to the book's methodology — show a close-up of a DNA fragment end with the "scar" highlighted, ancient fragments all bearing the mark, modern ones clean. The barcoding concept can be a small sidebar annotation. One strong visual beats two compressed ones.

**Page 14, Slot 3 ("Coverage"):**
Issue: This is acknowledged in the design notes as a "dry concept" but the visual — horizontal bars with overlapping fragments at different depths — is exactly how every genomics textbook presents coverage. For a graphic novel aimed at making science accessible, this is a missed opportunity.

Show Nityesh trying to hear a whispered word vs. hearing a chorus. This makes "coverage = confidence" intuitive without requiring the reader to understand what stacked horizontal bars mean. The technical diagram can be a small inset for readers who want precision.

**Page 14, Slot 7 ("The Factory"):**

The key emotional point (artisanal extraction from page 13 feeding into industrial-scale production) is stated in the notes but not in the visual description itself.

Instead of one scientist in one chamber, show ROWS of chambers, or a single wide shot where the intimate clean room from page 13 is visible as one station among many. **Page 15, Slot 4 ("A Genome in Search of a Fossil"):**

The "enormous molar teeth" illustration is mentioned but feels tacked on. ## 4. TEACHING CLARITY

### Accurate and clear

- **Page 13 (DNA degradation, petrous bone, clean room, extraction):** All technically accurate. The 30-80 base pair fragment size is correct. Petrous bone preservation advantage (~100x) matches the literature (Pinhasi et al. 2015). The three-chambered clean room suite with UV sterilization and positive air pressure is accurate. Chemical extraction steps (dissolve, bind to silica, wash, elute) correctly describe silica-based DNA purification. C-to-T deamination at fragment ends is the standard ancient DNA damage signature.

- **Page 14 (Sequencing, alignment, coverage, Four Population Test, factory):** Next-generation sequencing description is accurate. Coverage explanation is correct. The molecular clock callback to page 04 is a good structural choice. The Four Population Test (D-statistic / ABBA-BABA test) is accurately described — the diamond arrangement maps to the standard test topology. The "one in a quadrillion" stat comes from Green et al. 2010. Factory stats (~90 samples, under $500, 75% success in cold climates) match Reich's descriptions.

- **Page 15 (Denisovans, ghost populations, superarchaics):** mtDNA differences (~400 vs modern humans, ~200 for Neanderthals) are correct and come from Krause et al. 2010. The 800,000-1,000,000 year separation estimate for Denisovan mtDNA is correct. The 70% primate DNA yield is accurate (Reich describes this in the podcast and in his book). Denisovan ancestry in Melanesians/Aboriginal Australians (3-6%) is correct. EPAS1 Tibetan high-altitude gene from Denisovans is correct (Huerta-Sanchez et al. 2014). Superarchaic split at 0.9-1.4 million years with 3-6% contribution to Denisovans matches Jacobs et al. 2019 / Browning et al. 2018.

- **Page 16 (Species timeline, braided river):** Species dates and descriptions are all within accepted ranges. Homo erectus 1.9M-100K is correct. Neanderthal 400K-40K is standard. The braided river model is the correct framing per Reich's own emphasis.

### Science issues to address

**Page 14, Slot 4 — "~30 million differences" between modern human and Neanderthal:**
This number is too high. The actual figure is approximately 30 million TOTAL variant positions in the Neanderthal genome compared to the human reference, but the vast majority of those are shared with modern humans (they're common variants). The number of positions where Neanderthals differ from ALL modern humans is much smaller — on the order of ~100,000 fixed differences. The ~3 million differences between two random modern humans is correct. But comparing this to "~30 million" for Neanderthals is misleading — it implies Neanderthals are 10x more different from us than we are from each other, which overstates the genetic distance. The actual divergence is closer to a factor of ~2-3x more differences per base pair. Recommend: Either use a consistent metric (e.g., "divergence per base pair") or change the Neanderthal figure to something like "~12-15 million nucleotide differences" with a note that this includes both lineage-specific and shared variation. Or, simplest fix: remove the specific number and say "significantly more differences" — the RATIO matters more than the absolute number for the conceptual point being made.

**Page 15, Slot 7 — Superarchaic contribution direction:**
The spec says superarchaics "contributed 3-6% to Denisovan DNA." This is approximately correct per Jacobs et al. 2019, but the exact percentage varies by study. Some analyses (Browning et al. 2018) estimate ~1% contribution from a "superarchaic" source to Denisovans, while others place it higher. The 3-6% figure may be conflating two different signals. Recommend: Soften to "contributed a few percent to Denisovan DNA" to avoid false precision on a still-debated number.

**Page 15, Slots 5-6 — Denisovan ancestry geographic specificity:**
The spec says "Melanesia, Aboriginal Australia: 3-6% Denisovan ancestry." This is correct but incomplete — Denisovan ancestry is found more broadly across East/Southeast Asian and Oceanian populations, not just Melanesia and Aboriginal Australia (those just have the HIGHEST levels). The spec's framing implies it's limited to those regions. Recommend: Add a note that lower levels (0.2-0.5%) are found across East and Southeast Asia, with the HIGHEST levels in Melanesia/Australia. This prevents readers from thinking Denisovan mixture was geographically limited to those two areas.

### Potential confusion points

**Page 13, Slot 3 — "100x BETTER":**
The claim that petrous bone preserves DNA "100x better" is dramatic and attention-grabbing, but the actual improvement varies enormously by sample. Pinhasi et al. 2015 found up to 100-fold improvement in some cases, but the median improvement is lower. This is fine for a graphic novel — the "100x" is a real upper bound — but a sharp reader might question it. Consider: "up to 100x better" instead of a flat "100x BETTER."

**Page 14, Slots 5-6 — Four Population Test placement:**
The storyboard places the Four Population Test in page 14 as a methodology concept. But page 18 (Chapter 5, "The Encounter") in the storyboard describes it again: "The Four Population Test proved it — 'one in a quadrillion' chance of error." If the test is fully explained on page 14, page 18 becomes a callback rather than a revelation. This is actually GOOD structurally — teach the method first, then show its most dramatic application. But the page 14 spec already reveals the Neanderthal interbreeding result ("This is exactly what was found"). This means page 18's big reveal is already spoiled. Recommend: On page 14, teach the MECHANICS of the Four Population Test but withhold the result. Instead of "This is exactly what was found," say something like: "When this test was applied to Neanderthal and modern human genomes, the result was unambiguous." Save the emotional "tried desperately to explain it away" drama for page 18. This preserves the teaching without stealing the story beat.

**Page 15/16 overlap — The species silhouette:**
Page 15's hook panel (slot 8) describes silhouettes of all human species lined up, connected by flowing lines — "the braided river." Page 16 is entirely devoted to "The Cast Assembles" — the family portrait, the braided river, the species timeline. The slot 8 preview on page 15 undercuts page 16's opening impact. See Section 6 for the structural fix.

## 5. EMOTIONAL ARC

### Chapter 4 arc: "Reading the Dead"

**Page 13 (The Clean Room):** Awe at the technology. The reader enters a world they've never seen — spaceship-like clean rooms, dental drills on ancient skulls, the obsessive war against contamination. The Africa DNA gap plants an emotional seed that will grow throughout the book. Tone: reverence for fragile material, wonder at human ingenuity.

**Page 14 (Sequencing and Comparing):** Intellectual satisfaction. The "how it works" page — fragments become genomes, genomes become comparisons, comparisons become history. The Four Population Test is the intellectual peak. The factory concept adds scale. Tone: things clicking into place, the pleasure of methodology.

**Page 15 (The Finger Bone):** The emotional peak of the chapter and arguably of Part I. A child's pinky bone from a Siberian cave reveals an entirely new human species. The beer garden scene humanizes the scientists. Ghost populations introduce existential wonder — how many species lived and died leaving NO physical trace? The superarchaic reveal goes even deeper. Tone: escalating wonder, vertigo at the unknown.

**Page 16 (The Cast Assembles):** Grand synthesis. The family portrait pulls back to show the full scope of human diversity. The braided river replaces the simple tree. The before/after comparison delivers the meta-point: all of this was discovered in 15 years. The transition to Part II personalizes the stakes — "the encounter that changed YOU." Tone: awe at the big picture, personal stakes for what comes next.

**Arc assessment: Very good.** The chapter builds from methodology (pages 13-14) to discovery (page 15) to synthesis (page 16). The emotional trajectory is technology → method → revelation → big picture. This mirrors the actual arc of the field — you build the tools, apply them, and are stunned by what you find.

**One concern:** Pages 13-14 are both methodology pages with cool-background interview aesthetics. Two consecutive "here's how it works" pages risk flatness. Page 13 has inherent drama (the clean room visuals, the Africa problem), but page 14 is denser and more abstract (sequencing, coverage, the Four Population Test, the factory). The Four Population Test is critical and deserves the space, but the page needs strong visual variety to prevent methodology fatigue. The hook at the end of page 14 correctly injects thriller energy — lean into that transition.

**Connection to Part I:** This chapter closes Part I's toolkit arc. Chapter 1 taught what DNA is, Chapter 2 taught how it copies and shuffles, Chapter 3 taught the three histories in your body, and Chapter 4 teaches how to READ ancient DNA and what it revealed. Page 16 is the capstone — "you now have everything you need." The transition to Part II ("The Deep Past") is earned. The reader has been trained and is ready for the story.
