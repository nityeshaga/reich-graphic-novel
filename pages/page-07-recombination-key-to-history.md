## Goal of This Page

Connect recombination to ancestry — this is where biology becomes history. The reader should understand that your genome is a mosaic of ancestral segments, that recombination chops those segments shorter each generation, and that segment LENGTH tells you WHEN mixture happened. This is David Reich's core technique. Also: the Queen Elizabeth II example, why siblings differ, and why Neanderthal segment length matters.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-3 (top row, merged 3 slots) — "Your Genome Is a Mosaic"

A WIDE panoramic panel — the conceptual centerpiece of the page.

A genome shown as a long horizontal ribbon, color-coded by ancestor. Five generations shown stacked vertically, TOP to BOTTOM:

**Generation 1 (you):** A long ribbon that's approximately 50% one color (mom) and 50% another (dad), in BIG CHUNKS. Maybe 3-4 alternating segments per chromosome.

**Generation 3 (grandparents):** The same ribbon but now with FOUR colors (one per grandparent). The segments are SHORTER — maybe 6-8 per chromosome.

**Generation 5 (great-great-grandparents):** Now 16+ colors possible, segments visibly shorter — a patchwork quilt.

**Generation 20 (~500 years ago):** Many tiny segments from many ancestors. A fine-grained mosaic.

**Generation 2,000 (~50,000 years ago):** Thousands of microscopic fragments. And scattered among them — a few segments in a DISTINCTIVE color labeled "NEANDERTHAL." These Neanderthal segments are SHORT — much shorter than the recent ancestry segments.

An arrow running down the left side labeled: "EACH GENERATION: recombination chops segments shorter. ~1-2 cuts per chromosome."

The key insight in bold: "LONG segments = RECENT ancestry. SHORT segments = ANCIENT ancestry. Segment length = a clock."

Helix, standing at the bottom of the ribbon stack, gesturing upward: "Your genome is a patchwork quilt. Recombination cuts the fabric thinner every generation. By measuring the pieces, we can date when they were sewn in."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — "Dating Mixture by Segment Length"

A focused diagram showing how Reich uses this:

Two populations meet and interbreed. In the FIRST generation offspring:
- Long stretches from Population A (blue)
- Long stretches from Population B (orange)
- Clear, big blocks

After 10 generations: blocks are smaller
After 100 generations: blocks are tiny

Bold: "~1-2 cuts per chromosome per generation"

**The formula:** "Average segment length ÷ expected cuts per generation = generations since mixture"

Annotation: "This is the core PRINCIPLE behind Reich's dating methods. The actual math involves statistical decay patterns — but the intuition is exactly this: shorter segments = older mixture."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Queen and the Cave Man"

Two striking examples side by side:

**LEFT — The Queen Elizabeth II Paradox:**
A simple family tree showing that at 24 generations back, Elizabeth II has ~16.7 million THEORETICAL ancestors. But a callout reveals: only ~1,751 of them contributed ANY DNA to her.

"Most ancestral lineages get recombined out. After enough generations, a specific ancestor's DNA contribution drops to ZERO. You definitely descend from them — but you carry none of their DNA."

Annotation: "(estimated — the exact number depends on population structure and inbreeding in the royal lineage)"

Annotation: "Genealogy ≠ Genetic inheritance. You have far more ancestors than you have DNA from."

**RIGHT — The Oase Cave Man:**
A skull illustration with a genome ribbon beside it showing LONG Neanderthal segments — one spanning nearly a THIRD of a chromosome. The length is visually dramatic compared to the tiny Neanderthal fragments in modern people (from the top panel).

"Oase Cave, Romania. 40,000 years old. Neanderthal segments so LONG that his Neanderthal ancestor was within 6 generations. You can SEE it on the plot."

"Modern humans today: tiny Neanderthal fragments. Mixture ~50,000 years ago — 2,000 generations of recombination chopped them into slivers."

Nityesh: "So the LONGER the Neanderthal chunk, the MORE RECENT the interbreeding?"

Helix: "Exactly. The Oase man had a Neanderthal great-great-great-grandparent. You have thousands of Neanderthal ancestors — but 50,000 years of shuffling turned their contribution into confetti."

## Visual Design Notes

- WARM CREAM/CHALKBOARD BACKGROUND throughout
- The mosaic ribbon visualization in slots 1-3 is THE key image — it should be beautiful, immediately readable, and convey the concept that segment length = time. The progression from long blocks to tiny fragments should feel like watching a time-lapse.
- Use a WARM, DISTINCTIVE color for Neanderthal segments that stands out from the ancestry colors — perhaps a deep purple or burnt sienna. It should be instantly recognizable whenever Neanderthal DNA is referenced in future pages.
