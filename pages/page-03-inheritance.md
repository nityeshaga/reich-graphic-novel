# Chapter 1, Page 3: How You Got Your Genes (Inheritance)

## Characters

**Nityesh:** A pixel-art style young Indian man with distinctive big curly black hair, rectangular glasses, light stubble, white t-shirt, and beaded necklace. Pixel-art aesthetic — visible chunky pixels, retro game feel.

**Helix:** A small, friendly, anthropomorphic double-helix DNA strand. Coral/salmon pink strands with teal/cyan rungs (base pairs). Expressive cartoon eyes near the top, small stubby arms and legs. Standard comic style (NOT pixel-art).

## Goal of This Page

Teach Mendelian genetics with real depth: alleles, dominant/recessive, Mendel's laws, codominance, sex-linked inheritance. The reader should understand how traits are inherited and WHY some traits skip generations. This directly sets up why Reich cares about allele frequencies across populations.

## Panel Layout

### Slots 1-2 (top-left, merged 2 slots) — "The Monk and the Peas"

A warm illustration of Gregor Mendel in his monastery garden, surrounded by pea plants. He's depicted warmly — a curious, patient friar kneeling among tall pea plants with purple and white flowers. A small notebook in his hand with tally marks.

Caption: "1866. Gregor Mendel. An Augustinian friar in Brno (modern-day Czechia) who spent eight years crossing pea plants and counting the results. He discovered the laws of inheritance — 90 years before anyone knew DNA existed."

Below Mendel, a key concept box:

**ALLELES:** Different versions of the same gene. A gene for flower color might have a purple allele (P) and a white allele (p). You carry TWO copies of each gene — one from each parent.

**Homozygous** = both copies the same (PP or pp)
**Heterozygous** = two different copies (Pp)

Helix stands next to the concept box: "Everything Reich studies comes back to this: which alleles exist in a population, and how often."

### Slot 3 (top-center-right, 1 slot) — "Dominant & Recessive"

A clear Punnett square showing a cross between two heterozygous parents (Pp × Pp):

```
       P      p
  P   PP     Pp
  p   Pp     pp
```

Results shown with flower illustrations:
- PP = purple flower (homozygous dominant)
- Pp = purple flower (heterozygous — dominant masks recessive)
- Pp = purple flower
- pp = WHITE flower (homozygous recessive)

Bold: "3:1 ratio — the signature of dominant/recessive inheritance"

Annotation: "THIS is why traits skip generations. Two brown-eyed parents (Bb × Bb) can have a blue-eyed child (bb). The recessive allele was hiding."

### Slot 4 (top-right, 1 slot) — "Mendel's Laws"

Clean, bold text with small illustrations:

**Law 1 — Segregation:**
"Each parent randomly passes ONE of their two alleles to each offspring."
Illustration: A parent cell (Pp) splits into two gametes — one carries P, the other carries p. A coin flip.

**Law 2 — Independent Assortment:**
"Genes on DIFFERENT chromosomes are shuffled independently."
Illustration: Two pairs of chromosomes being sorted into gametes — eye color and earlobe shape inherited separately.
Small note: "Exception: genes on the SAME chromosome can be 'linked' — they travel together."

### Slots 5-6 (bottom-left, merged 2 slots) — "Beyond Simple Dominance"

Three mini-panels showing the complexity beyond Mendel's clean dominant/recessive:

**Panel A — Codominance (Blood Types):**
A visual showing blood type inheritance:
- Allele I^A + Allele I^B → Type AB blood (BOTH expressed fully)
- Two I^A or I^A + i → Type A
- Two I^B or I^B + i → Type B  
- Two i → Type O
Annotation: "A and B are codominant — neither masks the other. You get BOTH."

**Panel B — Incomplete Dominance:**
Red flower × White flower → Pink flower (intermediate blend)
"Some alleles don't fully dominate — you get a middle ground."

**Panel C — Polygenic Traits:**
A bell curve showing height distribution, with hundreds of tiny gene markers along the bottom.
"Most human traits — height, skin color, disease risk — are influenced by HUNDREDS of genes, each with a tiny effect. This is why Reich studies allele FREQUENCIES across populations, not single genes."

### Slot 7 (bottom-center-right, 1 slot) — "Sex-Linked Inheritance"

A diagram showing X-linked inheritance for color blindness:

- Mother (X^C X^c) — carrier, normal vision
- Father (X^C Y) — normal vision
- Possible children:
  - Daughter (X^C X^C) — normal
  - Daughter (X^C X^c) — carrier
  - Son (X^C Y) — normal
  - Son (X^c Y) — COLOR BLIND

Bold: "Males (XY) have only ONE X. One recessive allele = the trait shows up."
"Color blindness: ~8% of males, <1% of females."

Annotation: "This is why the X chromosome tells a different story in population genetics — it spends 2/3 of its time in females. We'll use this later to detect sex-biased migration."

### Slot 8 (bottom-right, 1 slot) — Hook Panel

Nityesh, looking satisfied but with a new question forming:

"Now I get how genes are passed down. But genes don't stay the same forever, do they? They must CHANGE somehow."

Helix, with an excited expression, gesturing dramatically:

"They do. And THAT — mutations, selection, drift — is where the story of human history begins. Everything David Reich studies starts on the next page."

## Visual Design Notes

- WARM CREAM/CHALKBOARD BACKGROUND — Helix teaching page
- Mendel's garden should feel warm and charming — not a sterile lab, a sun-dappled monastery garden
- Punnett squares should be clean, colorful, and immediately readable — not intimidating
- The blood type panel should use clear, distinct colors for each type
- The polygenic trait bell curve is important — it bridges from simple genetics to population-level thinking (which is what Reich does)
- Sex-linked inheritance diagram should clearly show why males are more affected — the single X with no backup
- All genetic notation (P, p, I^A, I^B, X^C, X^c) should be in a clean monospace or serif font, clearly distinct from caption text
